Aswan Governorate

Aswan Governorate is located in the far south of the country on the banks of the Nile River. It is known for its natural beauty and rich history and has been an important cultural center since the Pharaonic era. Aswan is famous for its archaeological temples and the High Dam, one of the most significant engineering projects of the 20th century. Aswan is a distinguished tourist destination, characterized by its mild winter climate and breathtaking natural scenery.

Landmarks and Tourist Attractions

Aswan, located in southern Egypt, is one of the most important governorates in the country. It is a major tourist attraction due to its location on the Nile, stunning landscapes, and unique archaeological sites. Its warm climate and botanical islands also make it special. It was a crucial city for the Pharaohs due to its strategic location.

. The ancient Egyptians called it "Suno," meaning "market," because it was a major trade center for many caravans. The name was later altered to Aswan.

Abu Simbel Temple

built by King Ramses II, is one of Aswan's most significant archaeological sites. It is famous for the Sun Alignment Phenomenon, which attracts global attention twice a year. The temple features four massive seated statues at its entrance, one of which collapsed due to an ancient earthquake, with its remains still on the ground

Philae Temples

Philae Temples are among the most notable archaeological sites, including several temples
dating back to the reign of King Thutmose III.

The most famous is the Temple of Isis, which remained in use until the Byzantine Emperor
Justinian I (527-565 AD) ordered the closure
of all pagan temples

Kom Ombo Temple

Kom Ombo Temple, dating back to the Roman era, has been central to archaeological
discoveries in recent years. It is dedicated to two
deities: Sobek, the crocodile god, and Horus the Elder, depicted as a falcon.

• Aswan is Egypt's primary source of water and electricity, home to the High Dam and its artificial
lake, the largest man-made lake in the world.

• The governorate is renowned for cultural and therapeutic tourism. The sand in Aswan is a natural
remedy for rheumatism and bone diseases.

• Few people know that Aswan housed Egypt's first military school in 1837, built by Mohamed Ali
Pasha. It was later converted into El-Tabia Mosque.

• Aswan Reservoir (Aswan Low Dam) has been in use for over 100 years to relieve water pressure
on the High Dam.

• The region includes Nubian archaeological sites, notably Amada and Wadi El-Sebua. Amada
Temple was built during the reigns of Thutmose III
and Amenhotep II.

• Qubbet el-Hawa, or "Dome of the Wind," is home to tomb carvings of noblemen and priests from
Ancient Egypt.

• Natural Reserves in Aswan: Saluga and Ghazal Islands Reserve and Wadi Allaqi Reserve.

• Aswan also contains significant Coptic and Islamic monuments, including St. Pachomius
Monastery and the Aga Khan Mausoleum
